What's in the database?

The database Hymn melodies. Danish reformation hymns 1529-1573 holds the melodies included in the Danish hymn and service books published online at salmer.dsl.dk.
The database is intended to illustrate relations between the melodies involved. Apart from the notated melodies themselves the database informs about their transmission and use during the 16th century. References to further reading and links to other digital resources provide access to further information.

Various types of database records describe the material at different levels. Record types and internal links are colour-coded as follows to guide the user:

Melody in a publication The melody and its text as it occurs in a particular hymn or service book.
If the melody does not occur in any other book within the edited corpus, also historical information about the melody is given at this level.
Melody General information about a melody occurring in more than one book within the edited corpus.
Conveys historical information about the melody and provides links to the records describing the melody as found in each of the books.
The various notations of the melody are shown as well for easy comparison.
Publication Publication records represent a single hymn or service book.
They offer a sortable list of the melodies included and may serve as a quick overview and an entry point to each of the books.

Links to the digital edition of the books are marked yellow with icons indicating whether they include notated music: Digital edition or not: Digital edition.

Interacting with scores

The menu icon next to each melody offers various kinds of interaction with the score:

Playback

Click the (play) button to listen to the melody. Notes are highlighted during playback to facilitate following the score.

MIDI download

Melodies are downloadable in MIDI format for playback locally on the user's computer or other devices. Click the MIDI icon in the melody menu to download.
This feature is available only in the melody database, not in the digital edition of the books.

Modifying the notation

Clefs

The melodies are often notated using clefs unfamiliar to many readers today. To improve readability, the clefs may be substituted for more current ones: G clef, octave-transposing G clef ("vocal tenor clef") or bass clef. Click the desired clef in the music menu to change.

Note values

The hymn and service books present the melodies using obsolescent forms of notation — square notation (rectangular, stemless noteheads), mensural notation or a hybrid form of both. Mensural notation resembles modern notation ("Common Western Notation", CWN), and the digital edition presents them using their equivalent modern note shapes. To the modern reader, the resulting long note values may be unfamiliar or misleadingly suggest a very slow tempo. To align the notation with current notational practice, note values may be diminished to half or a quarter of their original length in the music menu.
Please note: Reducing the note values causes the mensuration sign (corresponding to what would be seen as a "time signature" in modern notation) at the beginning to change colour to red, reminding the user that it is not to be understood as a time signature in the modern sense, but as a mensuration sign, which is not readily diminishable and therefore will be misleading in the context of diminished note values.

Transposition

The music menu allows transposing the melodies up or down as required. The playback feature reflects the current transposition.

Searching

Title search

Searching melodies by title is available on all pages by clicking the search icon in the upper right corner of the page.
Enter a title or part of a title in the search field or choose one from the expandable list below it. Titles may be entered using original or modernised orthography.

Melodic search

From the melody databases' search page also the music is searchable. The search page is available at https://melodier.dsl.dk/mei_search.xq. It is also reachable from any other page by clicking "Advanced search" below the title search field.
Due to the rhythmic variation of the melodies and the rhythmic ambiguity of some of the notational systems involved, all searches are rhythmic unaware and take into account only pitch.
The search page's tabs offer various ways of searching the melodies:

Searching by pitch name

Melodies are searchable using pitch names.
Altered notes are written as V (C sharp/D flat), W (D sharp/E flat), X (F sharp/G flat), Y (G sharp/A flat), and Z (A sharp/ B flat).

EXAMPLE: Searching for the pitch sequence CAFACDC finds the melody Nu er fød os Jesus Christ / Resonet in laudibus (In Denmark also known today as Lad det klinge sødt i sky).

Searching by melodic contour

A more flexible way of searching the melodies is by specifying the contour of the melody, that is, whether the melodic line is ascending (indicated by a /), descending (\) or repeating a note (-). Alternatively, the letters u, d and r may be used for "up", "down", and "repeat", respectively.
As the countour search takes into account only the direction of the intervals but is indifferent to their size, it yields many more results than the pitch name search. Therefore a somewhat longer query string will usually be needed to limit the number of results.

EXAMPLE: Searching for the contour \\///\-\\///\ finds – as above – Nu er fød os Jesus Christ / Resonet in laudibus.

Searching by notation (piano input)

Melodies are searchable also by means of music notation. The piano input allows sequences of up to twelve notes.

The search may be narrowed or broadened by a number of options:

  • All transpositions: Broadens the search by including results matching the note sequence in all transpositions (in essence, searching by intervals instead of pitches)
  • Starts with: Narrows the search to melodies starting with the specified note sequence
  • Ignore repeated notes: Broadens the search by treating repeated notes as one, thus enabling matches also where, for instance, notes are subdivided into two or more to fit differing numbers of syllables.
  • Precision: One or two edits (deviations) from the specified query sequence may be allowed. Missing notes, added notes and altered pitches each count as one edit.

EXAMPLE: Searching for the first eight notes of Vor Gud han er så fast en borg ("A Mighty Fortress Is Our God") in its modern Danish shape requires allowing two edits, because the fifth note diverges in pitch (D instead of E) and because the old form of the melody has an extra note (between F and D).

Melodic search is also immidiately available wherever there is music notation in the digital edition of the books or in the melody database: Click on a note and then on another to mark a melodic passage. Click the marked passage to perform a search for similar sequences (or click on a note outside the marking to unselect).
